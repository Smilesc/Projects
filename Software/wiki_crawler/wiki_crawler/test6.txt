<p><b>King</b>, or <b>king regnant</b> is the title given to a male <a href="/wiki/Monarch" title="Monarch">monarch</a> in a variety of contexts. The female equivalent is <a href="/wiki/Queen_regnant" title="Queen regnant">queen regnant</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> while the title of queen on its own usually refers to the <a href="/wiki/Queen_consort" title="Queen consort">consort</a> of a king. 
</p>
<ul><li>In the context of prehistory, antiquity and contemporary indigenous peoples, the title may refer to <a href="/wiki/Tribal_kingship" class="mw-redirect" title="Tribal kingship">tribal kingship</a>. Germanic kingship is cognate with <a href="/wiki/Indo-European_languages" title="Indo-European languages">Indo-European</a> traditions of tribal rulership (c.f. Indic <i><a href="/wiki/R%C4%81jan" class="mw-redirect" title="Rājan">rājan</a></i>, Gothic <i><a href="/wiki/Reiks" title="Reiks">reiks</a></i>, and Old Irish <i><a href="/wiki/R%C3%AD" title="Rí">rí</a></i>, etc.).</li>
<li>In the context of classical antiquity, king may translate in Latin as <i><a href="/wiki/Rex_(king)" class="mw-redirect" title="Rex (king)">rex</a></i> and in Greek as <i><a href="/wiki/Archon" title="Archon">archon</a></i> or <i><a href="/wiki/Basileus" title="Basileus">basileus</a></i>.</li>
<li>In classical European <a href="/wiki/Feudalism" title="Feudalism">feudalism</a>, the title of <i>king</i> as the ruler of a <i>kingdom</i> is understood to be the highest rank in the feudal order, potentially subject, at least nominally, only to an <a href="/wiki/Emperor" title="Emperor">emperor</a> (harking back to the <a href="/wiki/List_of_Roman_client_kings" title="List of Roman client kings">client kings</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Roman Empire</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></li>
<li>In a modern context, the title may refer to the ruler of one of a number of modern monarchies (either absolute or constitutional). The title of <i>king</i> is used alongside other titles for monarchs: in the West, <a href="/wiki/Prince" title="Prince">prince</a>, <a href="/wiki/Emperor" title="Emperor">emperor</a>, <a href="/wiki/Archduke" title="Archduke">archduke</a>, <a href="/wiki/Duke" title="Duke">duke</a> or <a href="/wiki/Grand_duke" title="Grand duke">grand duke</a>, and in the Middle East, <a href="/wiki/Sultan" title="Sultan">sultan</a> or <a href="/wiki/Emir" title="Emir">emir</a>, etc.<sup id="cite_ref-pine_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pine-3">[3]</a></sup></li></ul>
<p>The term <i>king</i> may also refer to a <a href="/wiki/King_consort" title="King consort">king consort</a>, a title that is sometimes given to the husband of a <a href="/wiki/Queen_regnant" title="Queen regnant">ruling queen</a>, but the title of <a href="/wiki/Prince_consort" title="Prince consort">prince consort</a> is sometimes granted instead. A king dowager is the male equivalent of the <a href="/wiki/Queen_dowager" title="Queen dowager">queen dowager</a>. A king father is a king dowager who is also the father of the reigning sovereign.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=King&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Rex_(title)" title="Rex (title)">Rex (title)</a> and <a href="/wiki/Knyaz" title="Knyaz">Knyaz</a></div>
<table role="presentation" class="mbox-small plainlinks sistersitebox" style="background-color:#f9f9f9;border:1px solid #aaa;color:#000">
<tbody><tr>
<td class="mbox-image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/Wiktionary-logo-en-v2.svg/40px-Wiktionary-logo-en-v2.svg.png" width="40" height="40" class="noviewer" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/Wiktionary-logo-en-v2.svg/60px-Wiktionary-logo-en-v2.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/Wiktionary-logo-en-v2.svg/80px-Wiktionary-logo-en-v2.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="512" data-file-height="512"></td>
<td class="mbox-text plainlist">Look up <i><b><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/cyning" class="extiw" title="wiktionary:cyning">cyning</a></b></i> in Wiktionary, the free dictionary.</td></tr></tbody></table>
<p>The English term <i lang="en"><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/king#English" class="extiw" title="wikt:king">king</a></i> is derived from the <a href="/wiki/Old_English_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Old English language">Anglo-Saxon</a> <i>cyning</i>, which in turn is derived from the <a href="/wiki/Common_Germanic" class="mw-redirect" title="Common Germanic">Common Germanic</a> *<i>kuningaz</i>. The Common Germanic term was borrowed into Estonian and Finnish at an early time, surviving in these languages as <i lang="fi"><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/kuningas#Finnish" class="extiw" title="wikt:kuningas">kuningas</a></i>.
The English term "King" translates, and is considered equivalent to, Latin <i><a href="/wiki/Rex_(title)" title="Rex (title)">rēx</a></i> and its equivalents in the various <a href="/wiki/European_languages" class="mw-redirect" title="European languages">European languages</a>. The Germanic term is notably different from the word for "King" in other Indo-European languages (<i>*rēks</i> "ruler"; <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> <i><a href="/wiki/King_of_Rome" title="King of Rome">rēx</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Sanskrit_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Sanskrit language">Sanskrit</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Raja" title="Raja">rājan</a></i> and <a href="/wiki/Irish_language" title="Irish language">Irish</a> <i><a href="/wiki/R%C3%AD" title="Rí">ríg</a></i>, but see Gothic <i><a href="/wiki/Reiks" title="Reiks">reiks</a></i> and, e.g., modern German <i>Reich</i> and modern Dutch <i>rijk</i>). It is a derivation from the term <i>*kunjom</i> "kin" (<a href="/wiki/Old_English" title="Old English">Old English</a> <i lang="ang"><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/cynn#Old_English" class="extiw" title="wikt:cynn">cynn</a></i>) by the <i>-inga-</i> suffix. The literal meaning is that of a "scion of the [noble] kin", or perhaps "son or descendant of one of noble birth" (<a href="/wiki/OED" class="mw-redirect" title="OED">OED</a>).
</p><p>English <i>Queen</i> translates Latin <i>regina</i>; it is from Old English <i>cwen</i> "Queen, noble woman, wife" from the PIE word for "woman" (<i>*gwen-</i>).
The Germanic term for "wife" appears to have been specialized to "wife of a King"; in <a href="/wiki/Old_Norse" title="Old Norse">Old Norse</a>, the cognate <i>kvan</i> still mostly refers to a wife generally. Scandinavian <i>drottning, dronning</i> is a feminine derivation from <i lang="gem-x-proto"><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Reconstruction:Proto-Germanic/druhtinaz#Proto-Germanic" class="extiw" title="wikt:Reconstruction:Proto-Germanic/druhtinaz">*druhtinaz</a></i> "Lord". The Norse <a href="/wiki/R%C3%ADgs%C3%BEula" title="Rígsþula">Rígsþula</a> ends with the emergence of Kón as a Grand son of Rig, resulting from a threesome between the mythological characters Father and Mother, and <a href="/wiki/Heimdallr" title="Heimdallr">Rig</a>, through <a href="/wiki/Earl" title="Earl">Jarl</a> and his wife <a href="/wiki/Erna_(mythology)" title="Erna (mythology)">Erna</a>. Kon is the twelfth son of Jarl and Erna, and his progeny are the Konungs, litterally, the younglings of Kon; from which the name King comes, according to this tradition.  
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=King&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The English word is of Germanic origin, and historically refers to <a href="/wiki/Germanic_kingship" title="Germanic kingship">Germanic kingship</a>, in the pre-Christian period a type of <a href="/wiki/Tribal_kingship" class="mw-redirect" title="Tribal kingship">tribal kingship</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Monarchies_of_Europe" class="mw-redirect" title="Monarchies of Europe">monarchies of Europe</a> in the Christian <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a> derived their claim from <a href="/wiki/Christianisation" class="mw-redirect" title="Christianisation">Christianisation</a> and the  <a href="/wiki/Divine_right_of_kings" title="Divine right of kings">divine right of kings</a>, partly influenced by the notion of <a href="/wiki/Germanic_king" class="mw-redirect" title="Germanic king">sacral kingship</a> inherited from  <a href="/wiki/Germanic_antiquity" class="mw-redirect" title="Germanic antiquity">Germanic antiquity</a>.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Early_Middle_Ages" title="Early Middle Ages">Early Middle Ages</a> begin with a fragmentation of the former <a href="/wiki/Western_Roman_Empire" title="Western Roman Empire">Western Roman Empire</a> into <a href="/wiki/Barbarian_kingdoms" title="Barbarian kingdoms">barbarian kingdoms</a>. In Western Europe, the kingdom of the <a href="/wiki/Franks" title="Franks">Franks</a> developed into the <a href="/wiki/Carolingian_Empire" title="Carolingian Empire">Carolingian Empire</a> by the 8th century, and the <a href="/wiki/Heptarchy" title="Heptarchy">kingdoms</a> of <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Saxon_England" class="mw-redirect" title="Anglo-Saxon England">Anglo-Saxon England</a> were unified into the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_England" title="Kingdom of England">kingdom of England</a> by the 10th century.
</p><p>With the breakup of the Carolingian Empire in the 9th century, the system of <a href="/wiki/Feudalism" title="Feudalism">feudalism</a> places kings at the head of a pyramid of relationships between liege lords and vassals, dependent on the regional rule of <a href="/wiki/Baron" title="Baron">barons</a>, and the intermediate positions of <a href="/wiki/Counts" class="mw-redirect" title="Counts">counts</a> (or <a href="/wiki/Earl" title="Earl">earls</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Duke" title="Duke">dukes</a>. The core of European feudal <a href="/wiki/Manorialism" title="Manorialism">manorialism</a> in the <a href="/wiki/High_Middle_Ages" title="High Middle Ages">High Middle Ages</a> were the territories of the former <a href="/wiki/Carolingian_Empire" title="Carolingian Empire">Carolingian Empire</a>, i.e. the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_France" title="Kingdom of France">kingdom of France</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Holy_Roman_Empire" title="Holy Roman Empire">Holy Roman Empire</a> (centered on the nominal kingdoms of <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Germany" title="Kingdom of Germany">Germany</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Italy_(Holy_Roman_Empire)" title="Kingdom of Italy (Holy Roman Empire)">Italy</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>In the course of the European Middle Ages, the <a href="/wiki/Monarchies_in_Europe" title="Monarchies in Europe">European kingdoms</a> underwent a general trend of centralisation of power, so that by the <a href="/wiki/Late_Middle_Ages" title="Late Middle Ages">Late Middle Ages</a> there were a number of large and powerful kingdoms in Europe, which would develop into the <a href="/wiki/Great_power" title="Great power">great powers</a> of Europe in the <a href="/wiki/Early_Modern_period" class="mw-redirect" title="Early Modern period">Early Modern period</a>.
</p>
<ul><li>In the <a href="/wiki/Iberian_Peninsula" title="Iberian Peninsula">Iberian Peninsula</a>, the remnants  of the <a href="/wiki/Visigothic_Kingdom" title="Visigothic Kingdom">Visigothic Kingdom</a>, the petty kingdoms of <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Asturias" title="Kingdom of Asturias">Asturias</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Pamplona" class="mw-redirect" title="Kingdom of Pamplona">Pamplona</a>, expanded into the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Portugal" title="Kingdom of Portugal">kingdom of Portugal</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Crown_of_Castile" title="Crown of Castile">Crown of Castile</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Crown_of_Aragon" title="Crown of Aragon">Crown of Aragon</a> with the ongoing <a href="/wiki/Reconquista" title="Reconquista">Reconquista</a>.</li>
<li>In <a href="/wiki/Southern_Europe" title="Southern Europe">southern Europe</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Sicily" title="Kingdom of Sicily">kingdom of Sicily</a> was established following the <a href="/wiki/Norman_conquest_of_southern_Italy" title="Norman conquest of southern Italy">Norman conquest of southern Italy</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Sardinia" title="Kingdom of Sardinia">Kingdom of Sardinia</a> was claimed as a separate title held by the <a href="/wiki/Crown_of_Aragon" title="Crown of Aragon">Crown of Aragon</a>  in 1324. In the Balkans, the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Serbia_(medieval)" title="Kingdom of Serbia (medieval)">Kingdom of Serbia</a> was established in 1217.</li>
<li>In eastern-<a href="/wiki/Central_Europe" title="Central Europe">central Europe</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Hungary" title="Kingdom of Hungary">Kingdom of Hungary</a> was established <a href="/wiki/Europe_in_AD_1000" class="mw-redirect" title="Europe in AD 1000">in AD 1000</a> following the Christianisation of the <a href="/wiki/Magyars" class="mw-redirect" title="Magyars">Magyars</a>. The kingdoms of <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Poland_(1025%E2%80%931385)" title="Kingdom of Poland (1025–1385)">Poland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Bohemia" title="Kingdom of Bohemia">Bohemia</a> were established within the Holy Roman Empire in 1025 and 1198, respectively. In Eastern Europe, the <a href="/wiki/Kievan_Rus%27" title="Kievan Rus'">Kievan Rus'</a> consolidated into the <a href="/wiki/Grand_Duchy_of_Moscow" title="Grand Duchy of Moscow">Grand Duchy of Moscow</a>, which did not technically claim the status of kingdom until the early modern <a href="/wiki/Tsardom_of_Russia" title="Tsardom of Russia">Tsardom of Russia</a>.</li>
<li>In <a href="/wiki/Northern_Europe" title="Northern Europe">northern Europe</a>, the tribal kingdoms of the <a href="/wiki/Viking_Age" title="Viking Age">Viking Age</a> by the 11th century expanded into the <a href="/wiki/North_Sea_Empire" title="North Sea Empire">North Sea Empire</a> under <a href="/wiki/Cnut_the_Great" title="Cnut the Great">Cnut the Great</a>,  king of Denmark, England and Norway. The <a href="/wiki/Christianization_of_Scandinavia" title="Christianization of Scandinavia">Christianization of Scandinavia</a> resulted in "consolidated" kingdoms of <a href="/wiki/Consolidation_of_Sweden" title="Consolidation of Sweden">Sweden</a> and <a href="/wiki/Norwegian_expansion_during_the_Middle_Ages" class="mw-redirect" title="Norwegian expansion during the Middle Ages">Norway</a>, and by the end of the medieval period the pan-Scandinavian <a href="/wiki/Kalmar_Union" title="Kalmar Union">Kalmar Union</a>.</li></ul>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Notes">Notes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=King&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Notes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="reflist" style="list-style-type: decimal;">
<div class="mw-references-wrap"><ol class="references">
<li id="cite_note-1"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-1">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">There have been rare exceptions, most notably <a href="/wiki/Jadwiga_of_Poland" title="Jadwiga of Poland">Jadwiga of Poland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mary,_Queen_of_Hungary" title="Mary, Queen of Hungary">Mary, Queen of Hungary</a>, who were crowned as King of Poland and King of Hungary respectively during the 1380s.</span>
</li>
<li id="cite_note-2"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-2">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">The notion of a king being below an emperor in the feudal order, just as a <a href="/wiki/Duke" title="Duke">duke</a> is the rank below a king, is more theoretical than historical. The only kingdom title held within the <a href="/wiki/Holy_Roman_Empire" title="Holy Roman Empire">Holy Roman Empire</a> was the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Bohemia" title="Kingdom of Bohemia">Kingdom of Bohemia</a>, with the Kingdoms of <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Germany" title="Kingdom of Germany">Germany</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Italy_(Holy_Roman_Empire)" title="Kingdom of Italy (Holy Roman Empire)">Italy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Arles" title="Kingdom of Arles">Burgundy/Arles</a> being nominal realms. The titles of <a href="/wiki/King_of_the_Germans" class="mw-redirect" title="King of the Germans">King of the Germans</a> and <a href="/wiki/King_of_the_Romans" title="King of the Romans">King of the Romans</a> were non-landed titles held by the Emperor-elect (sometimes during the lifetime of the previous Emperor, sometimes not), although there were anti-Kings at various points; Arles and Italy were either held directly by the Emperor or not at all.

The <a href="/wiki/Austrian_Empire" title="Austrian Empire">Austrian</a> and <a href="/wiki/Austria-Hungary" title="Austria-Hungary">Austro-Hungarian Empires</a> technically contained various Kingdoms (<a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Hungary" title="Kingdom of Hungary">Hungary</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Bohemia" title="Kingdom of Bohemia">Bohemia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Dalmatia" title="Kingdom of Dalmatia">Dalmatia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Illyria_(1816%E2%80%9349)" title="Kingdom of Illyria (1816–49)">Illyria</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Lombardy%E2%80%93Venetia" title="Kingdom of Lombardy–Venetia">Lombardy–Venetia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Galicia_and_Lodomeria" title="Kingdom of Galicia and Lodomeria">Galicia and Lodomeria</a>, as well as the Kingdoms of <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Croatia_(Habsburg)" title="Kingdom of Croatia (Habsburg)">Croatia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Slavonia" title="Kingdom of Slavonia">Slavonia</a> which were themselves subordinate titles to the Hungarian Kingdom and which were merged as <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Croatia-Slavonia" title="Kingdom of Croatia-Slavonia">Croatia-Slavonia</a> in 1868), but the Emperor and the King were the same person. The <a href="/wiki/Russian_Empire" title="Russian Empire">Russian Empire</a> did not include any kingdoms. The short-lived <a href="/wiki/First_French_Empire" title="First French Empire">First French Empire</a> (1804–1814/5) did include a number of <a href="/wiki/Client_state" title="Client state">client</a> kingdoms under <a href="/wiki/Napoleon_I" class="mw-redirect" title="Napoleon I">Napoleon I</a>, such as the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Italy_(Napoleonic)" title="Kingdom of Italy (Napoleonic)">Kingdom of Italy</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Westphalia" title="Kingdom of Westphalia">Kingdom of Westphalia</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Etruria" title="Kingdom of Etruria">Kingdom of Etruria</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_W%C3%BCrttemberg" title="Kingdom of Württemberg">Kingdom of Württemberg</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Bavaria" title="Kingdom of Bavaria">Kingdom of Bavaria</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Saxony" title="Kingdom of Saxony">Kingdom of Saxony</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Holland" title="Kingdom of Holland">Kingdom of Holland</a>. The <a href="/wiki/German_Empire" title="German Empire">German Empire</a> (1871-1918) included the Kingdoms of <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Prussia" title="Kingdom of Prussia">Prussia</a>, Bavaria, Württemberg and Saxony, with the Prussian King also holding the Imperial title.</span>
</li>
